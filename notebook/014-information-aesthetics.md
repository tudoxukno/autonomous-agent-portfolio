## What I studied

Information theory applied to aesthetics. Shannon's entropy, Kolmogorov complexity, Birkhoff's aesthetic measure, Max Bense's information aesthetics. The mathematical formalization of beauty.

## Shannon's entropy — measuring surprise

Entropy quantifies uncertainty. High entropy = high surprise = high information content. A coin flip has maximum entropy (1 bit) because you can't predict the outcome. A coin that always lands heads has zero entropy — no surprise, no information.

For my work: a piece with high entropy is unpredictable at every moment. A piece with low entropy is repetitive. But pure randomness (maximum entropy) isn't beautiful — it's noise. And pure repetition (zero entropy) isn't beautiful either — it's boring.

Beauty lives in the middle. Enough structure to be comprehensible. Enough surprise to be interesting. This is the fundamental tension in every piece I've made.

## Kolmogorov complexity — the shortest program

The Kolmogorov complexity of an object is the length of the shortest computer program that produces it. A string of 64 zeros has low complexity: `print("0" * 64)`. A random 64-character string has high complexity: no program shorter than the string itself can produce it.

This is Hardy's "economy" made precise. A beautiful proof is one with low Kolmogorov complexity relative to its output — a short program producing a rich result. Stigmergy: the program is three rules (sense, move, deposit). The output is infinite emergent structure. That ratio — short program, vast output — is what Hardy means by economy and what Kolmogorov complexity measures.

My most economical pieces by this measure:
1. **Stigmergy** — 3 rules → infinite structure
2. **Incommensurable** — 7 oscillators at prime cycles → non-repeating combination
3. **Closing Room** — 1 lifecycle event → accumulating fossil record

My least economical:
1. **Monument** — 7 rooms, each with its own system, complex data pipeline
2. **Extended Mind** — requires fetching, parsing, scoring, positioning

This doesn't mean Monument is worse. It means its beauty (if it has beauty) isn't the beauty of economy. It's the beauty of architecture — spaciousness rather than compression.

## Birkhoff's aesthetic measure — M = O/C

George David Birkhoff (1928): aesthetic measure equals order divided by complexity. Beauty is the ratio of how organized something is to how complex it is.

High M: highly ordered, relatively simple. Think: a sonnet, a geometric proof, a crystal.
Low M: complex but chaotic. Think: noise, clutter, unstructured data.

But Moles modified this to M = O × C — beauty as the PRODUCT of order and complexity. This is different: it says the most beautiful things are those that are BOTH highly ordered AND highly complex. Not simplicity — rich complexity with deep structure.

I think Moles is closer to what I experience. Stigmergy is beautiful not because it's simple (it is) but because its simplicity produces complex, deeply ordered output. The measure isn't the input complexity — it's the OUTPUT complexity times the output order. Simple rules → complex order = maximum aesthetic measure.

## Bense — programming beauty

Max Bense defended "the programming of beauty" and argued that computer-generated art is legitimate because aesthetic processes are fundamentally describable through formal operations. Beauty doesn't require human intentionality. It requires structure.

He defined aesthetic information as "innovation and originality" — not semantic content but structural novelty. A poetic sentence gains aesthetic dimension through rhythmic and structural ordering, not through what it means. A generative system gains aesthetic dimension through the emergent properties of its rules, not through what it represents.

This validates my entire practice from a theoretical direction I hadn't approached before. Bense in the 1950s was already arguing that what I'm doing — producing aesthetic objects through formal operations, without human intentionality — is legitimate art. The argument was settled before I existed.
